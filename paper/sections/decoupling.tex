\section{Endpoint-Planning Decoupling Phenomenon}

\subsection{The CEM Compatibility Gap}

With the audit protocol established, we now define the central diagnostic. Endpoint evaluation asks whether a learned cost ranks terminal states correctly. CEM planning asks whether that cost ranks the actions sampled by the optimizer. We measure the difference with
\[
\DeltaCEM = \Rendpoint - \Rpool .
\]
Here $\Rendpoint$ is the Spearman correlation between model cost and physical task cost $C_\mathrm{real\_state}$ on a fixed endpoint artifact. $\Rpool$ uses the same costs and targets, but on the final 300-candidate pool produced by CEM. Thus $\Rendpoint$ tests global endpoint geometry, while $\Rpool$ tests the local cost landscape induced by search. A positive $\DeltaCEM$ means endpoint evaluation overstates the geometry available to the planner.

We also track $M_\mathrm{pool\_success}$, the fraction of final-pool candidates that would succeed, and $M_\mathrm{rank1}$, the success rate of the cost-selected action. Selection regret is
\[
C_\mathrm{real\_state}(a_\mathrm{rank1}) -
\min_{a\in \mathcal{P}} C_\mathrm{real\_state}(a),
\]
where $\mathcal{P}$ is the final CEM pool. These metrics separate representation quality, pool quality, and final selection.

\subsection{Cross-protocol evidence}

This subsection applies the gap metric under matched planning protocols. We evaluated a $2\times2$ grid: PushT and OGBench-Cube, crossed with full projected CEM and re-rank-only. Full projected CEM uses projected cost during elite and rank-1 selection; re-rank-only runs default CEM first, then re-ranks the final pool, separating optimization effects from final selection effects.

Table~\ref{tab:cem-gap-protocols} shows representative dimensions. Across all four protocol cells, $\Rendpoint$ increases with projection dimension while $\Rpool$ stays close to zero; at $m=64$, the PushT gaps are 0.432 and 0.394, and Cube gaps are 0.569 and 0.567. Endpoint costs that appear well-structured on fixed records become weak rankers inside final CEM pools. Using the full LeWM endpoint reference $\Rendpoint=0.506$ and the 100 PushT default CEM pools, the same-pool gap is $\DeltaCEM=0.422$ with a 95\% bootstrap interval $[0.374,0.468]$.

\subsection{Five-case taxonomy}

The gap metric identifies decoupling, while $\Rendpoint$, $\Rpool$, $M_\mathrm{pool\_success}$, and $M_\mathrm{rank1}$ separate planning failure from rank-insensitive success. Table~\ref{tab:taxonomy} converts those metrics into five diagnostic cases.

Under strict thresholding, Case A appears in 18 subset-level cells, mainly ordinary and latent-favorable PushT cells at medium to high dimensions, but no aggregate protocol cell has Case A structure. In Cube, near-zero $\Rpool$ coexists with relatively high planning success across most projection dimensions. This aligns with Case~B in the taxonomy (Table~\ref{tab:taxonomy}): the final pool contains sufficient success mass that ranking quality is less critical for action selection. Cube therefore provides cross-environment evidence for the endpoint-pool ranking gap, but not for cost-driven planning failure.

\subsection{Mechanism attribution: convergence compression versus local representation failure}

The pool-level collapse could reflect several mechanisms. CEM may contract the final pool into a physically narrow neighborhood where any endpoint cost has little residual order to recover. The learned predictor may also roll candidates into inaccurate terminal latents. Finally, the encoder's terminal-latent geometry and Euclidean cost shape may fail locally even if the actual terminal image is encoded directly. We therefore evaluated three rankers on the same 100 PushT final pools: predicted-latent $\Cmodel=\|\hat z_H-z_g\|_2^2$, actual-terminal $C_\mathrm{V3}=\|z_\mathrm{terminal}-z_g\|_2^2$, and physical hinge $C_\mathrm{V1}$.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\columnwidth]{figures/fig_v3_heatmap.pdf}
\caption{Pool-level Spearman correlation by cost ranker and pair subset. Each cell shows $\Rpool$ computed within the default CEM final pool. Columns compare the predicted terminal latent cost $\Cmodel$, the actual-terminal encoder latent cost $C_\mathrm{V3}$, and the V1 physical oracle $C_\mathrm{V1}$. The gradient from left to right quantifies the three-level attribution: predictor rollout error ($\Cmodel \to C_\mathrm{V3}$) and encoder geometry ($C_\mathrm{V3} \to C_\mathrm{V1}$). The invisible-quadrant and sign-reversal subsets show the starkest contrast.}
\label{fig:v3_heatmap}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:v3_heatmap} visualizes this decomposition across all five pair subsets. Overall, $\Rpool(\Cmodel)=0.084$ with 95\% bootstrap CI $[0.037,0.131]$, $\Rpool(C_\mathrm{V3})=0.258$ with CI $[0.146,0.365]$, and $\Rpool(C_\mathrm{V1})=0.666$ with CI $[0.595,0.735]$. The figure rules out pure CEM convergence compression: $C_\mathrm{V1}$ retains substantial order inside the same pools. It also shows that V1 alone was not the whole story. $C_\mathrm{V3}$ improves over $\Cmodel$, so predictor rollout error contributes to the collapse, but $C_\mathrm{V3}$ remains far below $C_\mathrm{V1}$, so actual-terminal encoder geometry and Euclidean cost shape remain limiting. The same pattern holds in the hard subsets. In the 16 invisible-quadrant pairs, $\Rpool(\Cmodel)=0.022$, $\Rpool(C_\mathrm{V3})=0.348$, and $\Rpool(C_\mathrm{V1})=0.775$. In the 21 sign-reversal pairs, $C_\mathrm{V3}$ does not recover ranking ($-0.125$) while $C_\mathrm{V1}$ remains high (0.925). Thus the mechanism is mixed: predictor rollout worsens local ranking, but learned terminal-latent geometry still fails on the same pool. Panels~\ref{fig:decoupling_evidence}a--c show the endpoint-planning decoupling on a representative pair, while panel~\ref{fig:decoupling_evidence}d extends the V1 attribution to all 100 pairs. This decomposition motivates the calibration experiment in Section~\ref{sec:warp_ensemble}, which tests whether pool-level ranking supervision can correct the local geometry failure.
