\label{sec:warp_ensemble}

\subsection{Motivation}

Section~4.4 identifies terminal encoder geometry as the dominant bottleneck inside CEM pools. Replacing predicted terminal latents with actual terminal encodings improves $\Rpool$, so rollout error contributes, but the actual-terminal latent cost still remains far below the V1 physical oracle on the same candidates. Section~6 then shows that simple inference-time repairs do not close the gap. This raises a natural follow-up question: if the final pool already contains physically rankable structure, can a lightweight learned calibration of the local latent distance recover the lost ordering?

This question matters because standard endpoint evaluation never exposes the failing object. The corrective signal lives in pool-local rankings after CEM has converged, not in global endpoint pairs. A calibration experiment therefore tests the most optimistic inference-time version of the diagnostic: if simulator-scored pool labels are sufficient to repair selection, then the gap may be mostly local and post hoc. If not, the failure likely reflects pair-specific geometry that must be addressed during representation learning itself.

\subsection{Method}

We train a small residual warp on LeWM's 192-dimensional terminal latent:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{warp}(z)=z+\alpha\cdot \mathrm{MLP}_{\theta}(z), \qquad \alpha=0.05.
\label{eq:warp}
\end{equation}
The MLP has one hidden layer with 32 units, ReLU activation, dropout 0.1, and an identity-biased initialization. Training uses pairwise ranking supervision from simulator-scored pool labels, sampled from the top-50 candidates under $\Cmodel$, plus an identity regularizer that discourages large latent distortions.

To evaluate whether this calibration generalizes, we use a strict split. Pairs 0--69 are the training set. Ten warps are trained by ten-fold cross-validation \emph{within} those 70 pairs only. Pairs 70--99 form a completely held-out test set that is never seen by any warp during training. At inference, CEM search remains unchanged; only the final selector is modified. For each candidate, every warp computes a warped Euclidean cost, the candidates are ranked within each warp, and the ensemble either averages those ranks or votes over each warp's top candidates. Figure~\ref{fig:method_warp} summarizes the pipeline.

\subsection{Training-Set Results}

On the 70 seen training pairs, pool-level calibration clearly finds corrective signal. Using the nine warps that were trained on each pool, mean $\Rpool$ rises from 0.086 under Euclidean selection to 0.441 under rank-averaged warped selection. This is a large local improvement: the warped selector learns that many pools contain recoverable physical ordering that the original Euclidean metric suppresses.

That seen-pool gain is scientifically useful even though it is not the quantity of interest for generalization. It establishes that pool-level ranking labels are informative and that the residual warp family has enough capacity to exploit them. The remaining question is whether that calibration transfers to unseen start-goal configurations.

\subsection{Held-Out Evaluation}

Table~\ref{tab:warp-selection-holdout} reports the strict held-out comparison on pairs 70--99, where no warp has seen any test pool. The main comparison is Euclidean selection versus warp-ensemble rank averaging. The result is negative: Warp Ensemble Selection does not outperform Euclidean selection on held-out pairs. Success remains 13.3\% for both methods, and the exact paired McNemar test yields $p=1.0$. Mean regret is slightly worse for the warp ensemble (37.67 versus 36.96), with paired-bootstrap difference $+0.71$ and 95\% CI $[-0.19, 2.24]$.

The auxiliary baselines sharpen the interpretation. A simple Mahalanobis reweighting reaches the same 20.0\% success as top-10 warp voting. Random selection from the Euclidean top-30 matches that success as well and slightly improves regret. The held-out best single warp also fails to beat Euclidean. In other words, the learned calibration is not uniquely extracting transferable structure; several crude selectors perform comparably once the evaluation is truly unseen.

We also tested the matched planning setting that mirrors the earlier method claim: default CEM search on the held-out 30 pairs with three seeds, followed by either Euclidean rank-1 selection or warp-ensemble post-hoc re-ranking from the same final pool. This held-out CEM comparison is likewise negative. Default CEM succeeds on 20.0\% of runs, while post-hoc warp reranking reaches 18.9\%; the paired success difference is $-1.1$ percentage points with 95\% bootstrap CI $[-3.3, 0.0]$, and McNemar again gives $p=1.0$. Mean regret also shifts in the wrong direction, from 31.01 to 32.11.

\subsection{Interpretation}

The combination of strong seen-pool calibration and failed held-out transfer reveals something important about the geometry failure itself. The distortion between Euclidean latent distance and physical ordering is not governed by a single global correction that can be learned once and reused everywhere. Instead, the direction and magnitude of the misalignment vary across start-goal configurations. A universal learned warp can overfit the local distortions present in seen pools while failing to predict the distortions that arise on new ones.

This result reinforces the central diagnostic rather than weakening it. Pool-level ranking labels clearly contain corrective signal, and the V1 oracle in Table~\ref{tab:warp-selection-holdout} confirms that held-out pools still contain exploitable physical structure. What fails is the hope that an inference-time calibration layer can generalize across those local distortions. The implication is that the endpoint-planning gap is a structural property of the learned representation that likely requires training-time intervention, such as CEM-aware encoder regularization, local isometry objectives, or pool-conditioned representation learning.

The V1 oracle therefore remains the relevant upper bound. On the same held-out pools where Euclidean and learned calibrators remain near 13--20\% success, V1 reaches 43.3\% success with regret 4.97 and $\Rpool=0.800$. The physical signal is present; the learned geometry still fails to expose it in a transferable way.
