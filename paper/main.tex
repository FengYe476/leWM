\begin{abstract}
Latent world models often plan by minimizing Euclidean distance in a learned latent space, so endpoint ranking is commonly interpreted as evidence that the Cross-Entropy Method (CEM) optimizes a well-aligned objective. We audit this assumption in LeWM, a Joint-Embedding Predictive Architecture (JEPA)-style latent world model for goal-conditioned control from pixels, and find that endpoint ranking and CEM final-pool ranking can decouple. At projection dimension $m=64$, the CEM Compatibility Gap $\DeltaCEM$ reaches 0.43 on PushT and 0.57 on OGBench-Cube, while rank-1 planning success is 29.6\% and 56.0\%, respectively. The audit covers two environments, four protocol settings, and eight random-geometry controls with fixed artifacts, pre-registered decision rules, and documented framing resets. Four repairs fail under their locked criteria. Learned endpoint costs flatten the elite landscape, generic visual features underperform the trained encoder, simulator-assisted hybrid CEM requires at least a 20\% oracle budget to recover successes on hard pairs, and a search-selection split regresses by 8.3 percentage points. These failures localize the problem to the interaction between cost geometry and CEM dynamics. % REVISION: addresses W1
For LeWM-style latent planners that select actions by terminal latent costs under iterative sampling-based optimization, we recommend reporting pool-level and selection-level metrics alongside endpoint metrics.
\end{abstract}
